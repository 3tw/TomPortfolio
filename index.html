The beginning

March 2019, Ljubljana, Slovenia

The original idea was to record the hammer's force when extruding clay out of the cylinder.
For that I needed to create a hammer that would enable to measure precisely the exact force of impact for every hit.
Each device is made of a body (usually cylinder, sometimes a box) with legs attached on the sides and a mould on the bottom that determines how the piece (i. e. product) is formed. The body is filled with clay and a piston is placed on top. The hammer then lands on the piston and with every impact, clay is extruded out of the body in intervals.
During this process, I encountered some problems I had not expected. As more clay got extruded, even tough the force delivered remained unchanged, the intervals became longer because of the clay’s own weight.Then, the position of the piston is not always vertical because it is “fixed” to the body only with its head and the upper part begins to lean. It needs to be constantly supervised and readjusted into the correct position.The device and the hammer are separate units which means the device moves out of the centre of impact with each hit and also needs to be readjusted frequently.
The original idea was later abandoned due to the problems listed above. The focus was shifted to the creation of different devices (five in total) and exploration of possibilities to create different products, for example changing the diameter or even the shape of the container, changing the mould on the bottom, using clay with more/less water etc.

This is a prototype device that has a blade set under an angle to scratch and cut clay into thin layers. It consists of a metal frame, a box for the clay that has no cover and a moving bottom - by rotating the screw, the bottom moves up an down. The plate is placed on the two rails on top of the frame. The blade is mounted on the front of the plate.

Clay is carefully placed into the box by making sure there is no air pockets. It then raises until the blade can reach it. It is now set: the plate is pushed away from the box, clay is raised to the desired height and a scratch is made. With each scratch, clay starts to build up, forming different textures depending on how much clay had been raised between each scratch. At a certain height, clay is no longer scratched, but cut instead.
